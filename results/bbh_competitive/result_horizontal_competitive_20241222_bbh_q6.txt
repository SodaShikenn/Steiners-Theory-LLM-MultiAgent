Response: The answer to whether Drew ordering coffee on Tuesday caused the coffee shop to make a profit that day is "No." 

Here's why:

1. **Multiple Causal Factors**: The profit made by the coffee shop was due to the collective actions of all four customers ordering coffee. It is misleading to attribute the profit to Drew's order alone when Kylie's, Oliver's, and Jen's orders also contributed equally to meeting the threshold for profitability.

2. **Sufficiency vs. Necessity**: While Drew's order was sufficient to ensure the shop could make a profit (since only one order is needed), it was not necessary for the profit to occur on that day. Profit would have been achieved regardless if Drew had ordered or not, as the other three customers still placed orders.

3. **Causation Clarity**: Causation implies a direct link between an action and its result. In this scenario, Drew's decision to order coffee is one of several actions that led to the profit. Without isolating Drew's order from the others, it's inaccurate to say it caused the profit.

4. **Counterfactual Reasoning**: If we imagine a scenario where Drew did not order coffee, but Kylie, Oliver, and Jen still did, the coffee shop would still have made a profit. This further reinforces that Drew's order was not the cause.

In conclusion, while Drew's order contributed to the overall situation leading to profit, it did not singularly cause the coffee shop to make a profit that day. Thus, the correct answer is "No."
